Nyaa-kuza!!

    Nyaa-kuza!! is a narrative based fighting game where you play as an ex-yakuza cat, traveling through the outskirts of Japan in order to save your catnapped wife!
    Nyaa was my capstone project, it went through many iterations and rebrands before we published on Steam. My own role changed multiple times throughout the development cycle as it became apparent that the team was in need of strong leadership. I made the decision to put my team first and focus on being a producer-- a role I had on previous games-- rather than focusing soley on UX design, and I think it paid off tremendously. I didn't get to spend as much time iterating on and testing the UX as I would in a perfect world, but my team was grateful to have me at the helm as we consistently impressed our professors and classmates with our progress between sprints. When development began I was largely self-taught in UI/UX design, though during the last 3 months of development I began attending a UX/UI boot camp.

  • Main role: Producer
  • Secondary roles: Tech Lead, UX Designer, UI Engineer
  • Timeframe: 6 months
  • Platform: PC, Mac, Linux
  • Team size: 17

Pre-Alpha

Ideation

  We had lofty goals for Nyaa in the beginning. We envisioned a narratively-driven RPG with brawler elements, and the artists quickly created concept sketches that cemented the style and setting. Using those sketches and the discussions we were having as a team, I created a moodboard for the UI. I wanted to evoke the feeling of a Japanese city like Tokyo with the use of neon, paper lanterns, and textures from traditional kimonos and tattoos. Since our game was sure to have fighting and dialogue, I took UX inspiration from fighting games and role-playing games like the Naruto fighting games and Persona 5. The writers made many requests about how the dialogue UX/UI should look and function, and I endeavored to synthesize conflicting requests into a design that would satisfy everyone, but most importantly be intuitive to the player.
  As development continued, I began designing the fighting HUD. I originally envisioned the health bar as a large paw where each toe would act as a marker of health, then once I began considering a rage meter I updated my design to have the paw act as a single object. The rage meter was inspired by Doom Guy’s face in the original Doom; I wanted to show an increase in rage as well as change facial expressions. The inventory UI elements would change color as the weapons were used and entered a cooldown.

Choosing an Engine

  Originally, we had chosen Unity 3D to develop the game. We were able to build out a basic exploration level and combat level. We had decided to use an isometric view in the exploration level and a side-view in combat (inspired by fighting games). Much of the basic layout of the UX/UI was mapped out at this stage.
  It quickly became evident that Unity 3D would fight us most of the way, the physics of the isometric level was extremely floaty and difficult to wrangle, and level building took a long time as few on the team had prior experience with 3D level building. Switchibg was risky, but would save us a headache in the long run, so we scrapped the 3D version of the game and rebuilt it entirely in Unity 2D. The physics reacted better, though there were still problems, and level building was much faster.

Main Menu

  I laid the groundwork for the main menu during the 2D rebuild and the UI artist provided the first official UI assets for me to plug into the UI. At this time the team did not have an idea of what the background art for the main menus would look like, and so the layouts I designed were mostly placeholders.

Alpha

Menu Iterations

  The artist who illustrated our menu and dialogue backgrounds explained to me that he had a vision of the main menu: he would provide an illustration that the camera would slide down, finally coming to rest and the UI elements would fade in. He provided me with a rough illustration and the UI artists created our logo. I used these to design the final layout and build out the other menus.

Dialogue Iterations

  I rapidly iterated on the dialogue UX with the input of the writers. They referenced UI’s from games like Persona 5 and Stardew Valley, combining these two disparate UX layouts proved to be a bit difficult, so I created a number of mock-ups in the engine to show potential layouts given the inspiration. I also gave my opinion on which design would be most intuitive for the player. We finally agreed on a layout and I added the dialogue assets that the UI artist had finished.

HUD Implementation - Cutting Isometric View

  It became evident that, similar to Unity 3d, the isometric view was eating up a lot of time. The physics were still strange, and our artists had to produce twice as many assets. In the interest of protecting our time and creating a game we could be proud of, we decided to cut out the isometric view, switching the exploration levels to a side-view.
This is also when the engineers implemented the fighting HUD. Some of our planned mechanics had been dropped or changed, and after seeing how it was implemented I began brainstorming different designs.

Beta

HUD Iterations

  During my engineering-inspired brainstorming, I looked at many brawler-type games for inspiration, including Brawlhala and Speed Brawl. I sent a few rough sketches of the health/rage widget to the UI artist to get his input on what design he liked most. I also realized my original conception of the ‘inventory orbs’, as I had begun calling them, took too much space on the screen and had the potential to obfuscate the items displayed in the element, as well as simply not matching the assets from the UI artist. I took a screenshot into Photoshop and made a mockup of new inventory UI assets that I felt better fit the style and would not obscure the screen or objects inside them. I passed this design along to the UI artist and we discussed some alternate designs, including an accessibility feature to show the keyboard command for the weapon on the corresponding element.

Dialogue Assets

  The UI artist provided me with a wealth of dialogue assets, he gave me many different color palettes to choose from, I selected a simple all-pink, except for the ‘skip’ button which had been added by the engineers to make debugging easier, and had the added benefit of improving player experience.

Map

  Our illustrator provided me with an unfinished sketch of our map-- an isometric view of the game world as a callback to our earlier iterations of the game. I added this into the map that had previously existed only with programmer art, and set the buttons for travel above the corresponding areas, as well as added a sakura petal particle effect that another artist had created.

Release

Cutting Exploration

  Despite cutting 3D and isometric, the exploration level became worrisome. We had to maintain two separate character controllers and we did not have time to develop mechanics like quests that would make the exploration levels feel necessary or unique. We decided to once again cut down and remove the exploration altogether, instead using the dialogue scenes between combat levels to deliver story beats.

Controls Iterations

  We had gone back-and-forth on controls throughout all of development, and I still think they probably aren’t perfect. We were running out of development time, we had wanted to add re-mappable controls, but it was proving difficult to add in before our publishing date as we had lost three of our five engineers throughout development and there were still a lot of other engineering concerns to address. I suggested we instead add three types of control schemes for accessibility to make up for the lack of remapping, so we set to work on keyboard-only, keyboard and mouse (a much requested feature), and controller-only schemes.

Dialogue Iterations

  At this time, we received feedback that the UX of the dialogue was confusing to players. Due to F-shaped pattern reading, players would read the reaction before the main text. I also realized that the UI buttons were not all using the same color palette and wanted to set these to the same palette for consistency across the game. I set out redesigning the UX of the dialogue and I also added an animated arrow to indicate to the player that the main dialogue is a now button, as previously the writers had been using an extra dialogue button with ellipses to load the remainder of truncated text. I decided on a blue color scheme with yellow as a compliment since much of the game was pink and the yellow would stand out nicely against our game’s palette. At this stage we added in sprite animations to the dialogue UI which helped immensely with readability. I also added in the sakura particle effect and a dust particle effect to the background of the dialogue scenes to add some interest to an otherwise static scene.

HUD Iterations

  The initial rage meter used the same palette that our other assets used, including our environmental assets, meaning the element faded into the background and players usually didn’t notice when the rage was fully charged. I experimented with different colors and sent the results to the artists to get their input and they suggested a gradient between orange and yellow. I updated the color of the original asset in Illustrator and also updated the particle effects in the engine. This made a huge difference in visibility for the rage meter.

Map Iterations

  The illustrator finished the map asset, and once I implemented it, I realized the previous approach I had taken (plain text, no background), no longer worked on the more detailed background. I reworked the buttons to use the same button assets used throughout the rest of the game (later updated to reflect the color scheme of the dialogue buttons), and added a trail of paws between areas the player had already traveled between to increase readability.

Tutorial Iterations

  The tutorial level UI initially had the controls on screen with the current objective, however, as we added control schemes, it became apparent that keeping all controls on screen would take far too much screen real estate or time to figure out in code. I was inspired by Brawlhala’s tutorial screen which shows a short video paired with the controls to explain the game’s mechanics. I created similar screens, adding all control schemes next to the mechanic. The screen appears immediately as the player enters the level, and though the player cannot access the screen within the level after closing out of it, they are able to check the controls through the pause menu.

Results

Reflection

Working on Nyaa-kuza!! was a fantastic experience. I learned a great deal about fighting games, brawlers, dialogue in games, and I not only continued to learn about Unity, but had the opportunity to help teach my teammates about it. I especially learned about what it takes to publish a game and how to effectively lead a team.
Future opportunities: If I am ever able to revisit this project, I would like to add remappable controls and make the UI dynamically reflect the changes. I would also adjust some UI animations for accessibility (for instance, remove flashing/flickering for those with photosensitive seizures). I would also like to redesign the tutorial level to allow the player to easily access the example screens I designed from within the level, as well as the UI within the level to make it more friendly to controllers, since this was rushed at the end of development. The dialogue UI could use more help indicating which character is speaking, and I would also be interested in revisiting the design entirely to support scenes with more than two characters on screen. One concept I had not heard of during the development cycle is that of “red fatigue”-- that players can be overwhelmed by the amount of red on screen and become blind to it, leading to important UI callouts fading into the noise of the game. While we don’t use much pure red, we use a lot of pink, and I would like to revisit our color palette to avoid pinks so when we use them they are more effective.
Major takeaways from this project: I walked away from this game with a handful of extremely valuable lessons. I will summarize below:

  • Conflict resolution. My experience from my capstone showed me that things had generally gone well for me in past group projects. When my team realized there was interpersonal conflict, I reached out to someone who has experience in facilitating to help me manage the tension on the team. The rest of the team supported me and told me how much they appreciated my efforts. I want to learn more conflict management skills and strategies to de-escalate stressful situations.
  • What it means to lead effectively. I’ve been elected producer before, and now I feel I truly have an understanding of what that means. I have immense respect for anyone in a leadership role, and I would like to develop more skills in this area to help support my team.
  • The value of teamwork and how to achieve it. When I was elected to the producer position, I did what came naturally to me and pulled everyone together to make decisions and almost instantly the previous issues of siloing and feeling a lack of ownership were resolved. Collaboration and trusting our teammates’ expertise became an integral part of our momentum.
  • The point isn’t to make a game, it’s to make a team (and a game). You can’t make a game if you don’t have an effective team; if you invest in your team, they will deliver because they are invested as well. I spoke with others in capstone who were amazed at my team’s momentum and willingness to work and the only thing I can explain it with is that we respected each other and we didn’t want to let each other down, and it’s work we wanted to do-- for each other, as much as for ourselves.
  • The devil’s in the details. When the semester was coming to an end, it was all about “juice”, and that really hammered home to me that the difference between an obvious student game and a well-done indie game is really just in the details.
  • Iteration (and cutting content)! I’m baffled when I look back on how much we have iterated on our game, we would not have been able to deliver such a quality product without cutting basically 75% of what we had planned. We grew comfortable cutting content on this team, if something might not get done in time we would always bring it up as a possibility, usually that didn’t have to happen, but we were prepared, and I think that will do us well moving forward.
